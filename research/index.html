Electronic and magnetic properties of matter

My research focuses on the electronic structure and properties of complex quantum materials ranging from semiconductors, topological insulators, low-dimensional systems to unconventional magnets, with particular interest in the non-equilibrium behaviours derived from the many-body interactions that underpin their physical properties. My aim is to understand the fundamental nature of the exotic phases in these materials, and manipulate their electronic and collective phenomena at the ultrafast timescale in view of applications in the fabrication of devices with improved performances. To this aim, I primarily employ photoemission spectroscopy-based techniques as sensitive probes of the behaviour of electrons in solids. My activity is carried out in large-scale user facilities as well as in tabletop laser laboratories, pioneering the exploitation of non-linear phenomena such as high-harmonics generation (HHG) for femtosecond-resolved spectroscopies.

Low-dimensional materials

My research focuses on the study of the electronic properties in 2D magnets: I investigate orbital ordering, spin configuration, and fundamental excitations, to better understand how to harness their unique properties in view of applications in advanced technologies. One main target is the interplay between spin, valley, and layer degrees of freedom, together with dimensionality effects. Strong spatial confinement exacerbates Coulomb interactions, affecting the degree of electron-electron correlations and supporting rich exciton physics even at room temperature. This phenomenology becomes even more complex and exciting when stacking single layers of different 2D materials: properties resulting from interface effects, such as band alignment or charge-transfer phenomena, are strongly material-dependent, with a lot of wiggle room for tailoring the desired properties. Additionally, the emergence of effects such as moir√© band folding is stacking-dependent, and the twist angle between atomic layers becomes another powerful control knob. I aim to study the fundamental exciton properties (binding energy, lifetime, localization) and how they interact with orbital, valley, and spin ordering. The unique set of parameters available in a complete photoemission experiment allows also to reconstruct key phase information pertaining to the complex-valued Bloch functions underlying electronic orbitals, turning my approach into an ultrafast wavefunction recorder. of particular interest for applications towards next-generation devices.
